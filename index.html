Every language is a deal: how much control you keep versus how much the machine hands back. This plots the popular ones by how close they sit to the hardware and how they run, then says plainly what each is good at and when to reach for it. Hover the map, filter by what you're building, or scroll the cards.

What are you building?

Filter the deck to the languages that actually fit the job.

There is no "best" language, only the right deal for the job. Assembly gives you everything and protects you from nothing. Python protects you from almost everything and costs you speed. Haskell makes the compiler prove you right before it runs. The map is the trade-off; the cards are the receipts.

Honest footnote: HTML and CSS describe a document and how it looks, and SQL describes what data you want. None of them loop, branch and compute like the rest, so calling them programming languages is loose talk. They still earn their place in the stack. Also left off as targets rather than languages you hand-write: WebAssembly (a compile target) and Solidity (smart contracts, a niche of its own).
